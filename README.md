# The Institutionalization of Elastic Currency

**A research thesis on how the Federal Reserve shifted financial risk from private banks to the public domain — and what that architecture means for American money.**

## What this is

This is an interactive, single-page research essay examining one of the most consequential institutional decisions in American economic history: the creation of the Federal Reserve in 1913.

The thesis argues that the Federal Reserve Act did not simply solve the inelasticity problem of the pre-1913 banking system — it transferred the *cost* of that solution to the public. Banks retained the upside of elastic credit creation. The public absorbed the downside.

The piece is structured in three modules and a conclusion:

**Module 1 — The crisis that broke the old system**
Traces the 1907 Panic, the Jekyll Island meeting, and why the inelastic money supply produced eight major banking collapses between 1863 and 1913.

**Module 2 — How banks create money from lending**
Explains fractional reserve banking, the money multiplier, and how the Fed's discount window removed the last hard constraint on private credit expansion.

**Module 3 — The asymmetry: who profits and who pays**
Examines the risk-reward structure the Federal Reserve institutionalized — privatized gains during expansions, socialized losses during contractions — with the 2008 bailout as the clearest modern case.

**Conclusion — The architecture of transferred risk**
Frames the central question the original architects never answered: who should bear the cost of financial stability?
